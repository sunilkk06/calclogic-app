Complete Button Reference Guide

CalcLogic's scientific calculator includes every function you need for advanced mathematics, science, and engineering. Here's what every button does — including the ones that confuse people most.

sin cos tan Trigonometry

Calculates sine, cosine, and tangent of an angle. Switch between Deg and Rad mode depending on your problem. Essential for geometry, physics, and engineering.

log Logarithm (base 10)

The common logarithm. log(100) = 2 because 10 squared equals 100. Used in chemistry (pH), acoustics (decibels), and earthquake measurement (Richter scale).

ln Natural Logarithm

Logarithm with base e (2.71828). Used in calculus, compound interest, population growth, radioactive decay, and statistics.

x² Square

Raises a number to the power of 2. Example: 9 squared = 81. Shortcut for multiplying a number by itself — faster than using the power button.

x³ Cube

Raises a number to the power of 3. Example: 4 cubed = 64. Common in volume calculations and polynomial equations.

xʸ Power

Raises x to any power y. Enter base, press xʸ, enter exponent, press equals. Example: 2 to the power of 10 = 1,024.

√ Square Root

Finds the square root of a number. Example: square root of 225 = 15. For other roots, use xʸ with a fractional exponent.

1/x Reciprocal

Divides 1 by the displayed number. Example: 1/8 = 0.125. Useful in optics, electronics, and harmonic calculations.

exp Scientific Notation

Enters a power of 10. Example: 3 exp 6 = 3,000,000 (3 × 10⁶). Essential for very large or very small numbers in physics and chemistry.

mod Modulus

Returns the remainder after division. Example: 17 mod 5 = 2. Widely used in programming, cryptography, and number theory.

π Pi

Inserts pi (3.14159...). Used in all circle, sphere, and wave calculations. Example: area of circle = pi times radius squared.

e Euler's Number

Inserts e (2.71828...). The base of the natural logarithm. Fundamental to compound interest, growth and decay, and probability.

Degrees vs Radians — Which Mode Should You Use?

This is the most common source of wrong answers on a scientific calculator. Choosing the wrong angle mode gives a completely different result for any trigonometric function.

📌 Simple rule: Use Degrees for school geometry, navigation, construction, and everyday problems. Use Radians for calculus, physics equations, and university-level mathematics.
ConceptDegreesRadians
Full circle360°2π (≈ 6.283)
Half circle180°π (≈ 3.14159)
Quarter circle90°π/2 (≈ 1.5708)
sin(30° or π/6)0.50.5
cos(60° or π/3)0.50.5
tan(45° or π/4)11
Conversion formulaDegrees × π ÷ 180 = RadiansRadians × 180 ÷ π = Degrees
📘 Why mode matters — a real example sin(30) in Degrees mode = 0.5 ✅ (correct for a 30-degree angle)
sin(30) in Radians mode = -0.988 ❌ (wrong — 30 radians is a very different angle)

Always check your mode before any trig calculation.

Logarithms Explained: log vs ln

Logarithms are the inverse of exponents — they answer the question "what power do I raise the base to in order to get this number?" The calculator has two logarithm functions, and confusing them is a common mistake.

log(x) = log base 10 of x

# log(1000) = 3 because 10³ = 1000
# log(100) = 2 because 10² = 100
# log(10) = 1 because 10¹ = 10

ln(x) = log base e of x (natural logarithm)
# ln(e) = 1 because e¹ = e
# ln(1) = 0 because e⁰ = 1
# ln(7.389) ≈ 2 because e² ≈ 7.389

Real-world uses of log and ln

FunctionReal-World ApplicationExample

logDecibels in acousticsdB = 10 × log(power ratio)
logRichter earthquake scaleEach point = 10× increase in amplitude

How to Use Memory Buttons for Multi-Step Calculations

Memory buttons are one of the most underused features on scientific calculators. They eliminate the need to write down intermediate results and dramatically reduce errors in complex, multi-step calculations.

ButtonFull NameWhat It Does
M+Memory AddAdds the current displayed value to whatever is stored in memory
M-Memory SubtractSubtracts the current displayed value from whatever is stored in memory
MRMemory RecallDisplays the value currently stored in memory
MCMemory ClearErases the memory — sets stored value back to zero
📘 Real example — calculating total area of multiple shapes Step 1: Calculate area of rectangle → 12 × 8 = 96 → press M+ (memory now holds 96)
Step 2: Calculate area of triangle → 0.5 × 6 × 4 = 12 → press M+ (memory now holds 108)
Step 3: Calculate area of circle → π × 3² = 28.27 → press M+ (memory now holds 136.27)
Step 4: Press MR to display total area = 136.27
Step 5: Press MC to clear memory for next calculation

Order of Operations — How the Calculator Processes Expressions

Scientific calculators follow the standard mathematical order of operations — often remembered by the acronym BODMAS (Brackets, Orders, Division, Multiplication, Addition, Subtraction) or PEMDAS in the US.

PriorityOperationExampleResult
1stBrackets / Parentheses(3 + 5) × 216 (not 13)
2ndExponents / Powers2 + 3²11 (not 25)
3rdMultiplication & Division6 + 4 × 214 (not 20)
4thAddition & Subtraction10 - 3 + 29
💡 When in doubt — use parentheses If you're unsure whether the calculator will process your expression in the right order, wrap the parts you want calculated first in parentheses. Extra parentheses never cause errors and always guarantee the correct order.

Scientific Notation — Using the EXP Button

Scientific notation expresses very large or very small numbers as a value multiplied by a power of 10. It's essential in physics, chemistry, astronomy, and engineering where numbers like the speed of light (300,000,000 m/s) or the mass of an electron (0.00000000000000000000000000000091 kg) are common.

Scientific notation: A × 10^n

# Speed of light: 3 × 10⁸ m/s → enter: 3 exp 8
# Avogadro's number: 6.022 × 10²³ → enter: 6.022 exp 23
# Electron charge: 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ → enter: 1.602 exp -19
📘 How to enter scientific notation To enter 6.022 × 10²³ (Avogadro's number):
1. Type 6.022
2. Press exp
3. Type 23
4. Press =

The calculator displays: 6.022e+23

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate sin, cos, and tan?

First check your angle mode — press Deg for degrees or Rad for radians. Then press the sin, cos, or tan button and enter your angle value. For example, sin(30) in degrees = 0.5. For inverse trig (finding the angle from a ratio), most scientific calculators use a second function key — on this calculator, use the xʸ button with the appropriate expression.

What is the difference between Deg and Rad mode?

Degrees and radians are two units for measuring angles. A full circle equals 360 degrees or 2π radians. Use Degrees for everyday geometry, school problems, and navigation. Use Radians for calculus, physics, and advanced mathematics. Getting this wrong is the most common cause of incorrect trig results — always verify your mode before calculating.

What is the difference between log and ln?

log is the base-10 logarithm (common log). log(1000) = 3 because 10³ = 1000. ln is the natural logarithm with base e (2.71828). ln(e) = 1. Use log for chemistry, acoustics, and general calculations. Use ln for calculus, compound interest, and natural growth or decay problems.

How do I calculate square roots and cube roots?

For square roots, press the √ button and enter your number. For cube roots, use the xʸ button with an exponent of 0.333 (which equals 1 divided by 3). For example, to find the cube root of 125: enter 125, press xʸ, enter 0.333, press equals to get approximately 5. For the nth root, use an exponent of 1 divided by n.

How do I use memory buttons?

Memory buttons store values between calculations. M+ adds the current value to memory. M- subtracts it. MR recalls the stored value. MC clears memory. This is especially useful for multi-step problems where you need to use an intermediate result later — store it with M+ instead of writing it down.

How do I enter scientific notation?

Use the exp button to enter scientific notation. Type the base number, press exp, then type the exponent (power of 10). For example, 3 × 10⁸ = enter 3, press exp, enter 8. For negative exponents (small numbers), enter a negative sign before the exponent number.

What does the mod button do?

The mod button calculates the modulus — the remainder after integer division. For example, 17 mod 5 = 2 (because 17 = 3 × 5 + 2). Modulus is widely used in programming and number theory. A quick test: any number mod 2 equals 0 if even, or 1 if odd.

Why is tan(90°) undefined?

Tangent is defined as sin divided by cos. At 90 degrees, cos = 0, and dividing by zero is mathematically undefined — it approaches infinity. This is why calculators show an error or a very large number for tan(90). The same applies to tan(270°). This is a mathematical reality, not a calculator bug.
